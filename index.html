Executive LULC Transition Overview

Overall, Land Use and Land Cover (LULC) change from 2017 to 2023 in the Sri Sathya Sai District (as highlighted in mandal-level studies for Hindupur and Dharmavaram) is characterized by immense pressure on agricultural land. This pressure originates symmetrically from chronic drought leading directly to fallow/rangeland conversion, and simultaneous urban built-up expansion.

We've observed highly dynamic transitions among crops, rangeland, bare ground, and small water bodies. These transitions are strongly dictated by volatile rainfall variability and persistent, chronic water stress across the aquifer.

Rainfall Distribution

  • • Annual rainfall ranged between 600 mm and 770 mm.
  • • Broadly consistent with long-term normal (590–740 mm) but indicates spatial volatility.
  • • Supplied the foundational base for evapotranspiration, runoff, and water balance planning.

Surface Runoff

  • • Exceptionally high runoff ranging from 530 mm to 680 mm yearly.
  • • Vast majority of precipitation becomes surface flow rather than penetrating soil.
  • • Highest in barren lands, steep hills, and built-up infrastructure. Conservation structures are strictly necessary.

Recharge & Water Table

  • • Catastrophically low groundwater recharge: Only 5 mm to 19 mm yearly infiltration.
  • • Blamed on hard geological rocks, uneven topography, and eroded soils.
  • • Groundwater levels currently sit at 6 to 11 meters below ground, signaling immense over-extraction during dry phases.

Crop Water Demand

  • • Water requirements for local crops vary vastly from 127 mm to 510 mm.
  • • District-wide water balance spans from -22 mm to -444 mm, meaning 100% of the district operates in a deficit.
  • • Underscores an absolute mandate for demand-management and shift to less water-intensive crops.

Intervention Prioritization

Villages and mandals have been categorized into three distinct priority zones based on the mathematical separation between available rainfall and absolute crop demand.

Structural & Geological Factors

Drainage & Lineaments: Networks control surface runoff. Lineament fractures increase subsurface permeability identifying prime recharge zones.

Combining lithology, drainage, and fractures ensures NRM structures (check dams, contour bunds) are perfectly placed.

3

Priority 3 (Highest Urgency)

Rainfall << Demand

  • • Heavily dominated by large rainfed cropped areas and fallow/barren land.
  • • Severely limited irrigated capabilities (HNSS lacking); catastrophic dependency on volatile rainfall.
  • • LULC signals maximum potential for immediate structural interventions: farm ponds, recharge structures, and profound soil conservation.
2

Priority 2 (Medium Urgency)

Rainfall slightly < Demand

  • • Region exhibits a mix of rainfed and irrigated agriculture with scattered fallow patches.
  • • Moderate stress indicators: Cropping survives but fails drastically in dry years.
  • • Interventions focus on drastically improving irrigation efficiency and expanding localized recharge pockets.
1

Priority 1 (Stable)

Rainfall ≥ Demand

  • • Stable LULC transition profiles with reliable active water bodies.
  • • Advanced irrigated agriculture mitigating immediate local risks.
  • • Water balance registers near zero or slightly positive alignment.

Strategic Implementation Agenda

Micro-Level Database Targeting

Deploying comprehensive village-level databases assessing potential irrigable area ratios against actual Handri-Neeva (HNSS) Canal command coverage.

Targeted Recharge Construction

Integrating lineament lengths (classified in 5 subsets), Bhuvan network structures, and geological indicators to forcefully prescribe check dam and farm pond placements.

Canal Inefficiency Remediation

Isolating regions residing inside canal command areas that are failing to utilize resources efficiently, alongside aiding villages situated entirely outside the command network suffering acute depletion.
